Water In Our Schools

An article by Nicole Uzhca

Effects of Flint, Michigan Incident

In 2014, the city of Flint, Michigan, changed its source of water from Detroit to the Flint River. Unfortunately, the old pipes were not changed nor fixed. As a result, lead starting leaking into the water supply reaching levels ten times higher than the limit set by The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).

After the incident in Flint, Michigan, the Department of Education has taken significant measures to prevent further cases that involve high levels of lead in drinking water. Although lead testing is still not required by state law, the DOE has taken precautions and has found different approaches in addressing the potential risks of lead poisoning in public schools.

The DOE in Action

Various schools within the district have not been tested for lead in a total of ten years. Consequently, in 2014, 840 students younger than six years old were identified with high levels of lead in their blood resulting in long term effects; including but not limited to brain damage, growth impairment, behavior problems (ADHD), learning disabilities, infertility. Therefore, the DOE has changed and repaired 1,109 fixtures out of a total of 1,194 that tested for high levels of lead. The remaining 85 are in the process of being replaced.

Without a doubt, changing fixtures would prevent future corrosion in the pipes infrastructure. On the other hand, water resting in fountain pipes have a greater chance to contain lead. Moreover, parents throughout NYC believe that the DOE has not taken action in addressing the failing water system. In fact, they strongly believe that the DOE should provide free bottled water especially since 33,000 water fountains and faucets have been tested positive for lead since December 2016. Overall, the DOE has received multiple petitions concerning lead in drinking water.
